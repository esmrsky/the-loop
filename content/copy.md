# The Loop — full site copy (v1, editorial master)

> This is the red-pen document. Every word on the site lives here first.
> `{need}` and `{agent}` are placeholders filled by the visitor's picks (defaults: worth, scrolling).
> Structure: 8 acts. Dark and tightening through Act 4.5, breaking into light at Act 5.

---

## Act 0 — The hook

**H1:** Why do I keep doing the thing I hate?

**Vignettes** (fade in one by one):
- It's 1 a.m. Your thumb is still moving.
- You closed the tab. You opened it again.
- "Last one," you said. An hour ago.
- You promised yourself this morning. It isn't morning anymore.

**Body:** This isn't a story about weak people. It's a story about a shape. Almost two thousand years ago, someone wrote down maybe the most honest sentence ever recorded about being human:

**Quote:** "I do not understand what I do. For what I want to do, I do not do — but what I hate, that I do."
**Attribution:** — written around AD 57

**Body:** He didn't have the brain science. He had the experience. Keep going. The shape has a name.

## Act 1 — The need

**H2:** It starts with something good.

**Body:** Underneath every loop there is a need — and the need is not the problem. You were built to run on rest, comfort, worth, closeness, safety, joy. Needing them is not a malfunction. Hunger is not a moral failure.

**Body:** But a need that stays unmet doesn't politely wait. It hurts. And pain looks for the nearest exit.

**Prompt:** Which one feels emptiest lately?
**Privacy note:** Your answer stays on this page. Nothing is stored, nothing is sent.
**Options:** rest · comfort · worth · closeness · safety · joy

## Act 2 — The subscription

**H2:** So you subscribed to something.

**Body:** Not on purpose. Nobody signs a contract. But somewhere along the way you found something that promised relief from the ache of {need} — fast, reliable, no questions asked.

**Prompt:** What's your usual exit?
**Options:** scrolling · porn · food · a drink · work · approval · shopping · gaming

**Panel — the fine print:**
- The promise — relief, now.
- The delivery — real relief. For about eleven minutes.
- The price — goes up every month.
- Renewal — automatic.

**Body:** Here's the part nobody tells you: it works. That's why you chose it. You weren't stupid and you weren't broken — you were in pain, and {agent} worked. That's the trap. Counterfeits only sell because they resemble the real thing.

## Act 3 — The loop

**H2:** Watch what it builds.

**Scroll steps** (the diagram assembles, one node per step):
1. The ache of {need} shows up. Uninvited.
2. It becomes a pull. A craving with your name on it.
3. You do the thing. {agent}.
4. And relief comes. Real, warm — and brief.
5. Then the bill arrives: guilt. Shame. *Why am I like this?*
6. And shame hurts. Which is a fresh ache. Which is exactly where we started.

**Interactive:** [ Run the loop ] — each pass: the arrows thicken, relief shrinks, shame swells, it speeds up.
**Meta line:** passes: {n} · relief: shrinking · shame: growing
**After 4+ passes:** (you can stop — it won't.)

**Body:** Neuroscientists have a phrase: *neurons that fire together, wire together.* Every pass lays asphalt. This is why it stops feeling like a choice — you built a road, and roads are for driving. The loop doesn't need you to love it. It only needs you to come back.

## Act 4 — Willpower

**H2:** So you fight it. You cut the wire.

**Interactive:** [ Use willpower ]

**Beat 1 (revealed on click):** You white-knuckle it. The behavior stops… and the pressure doesn't. The need is still unmet. The ache builds behind the dam.

**Beat 2:** …and the loop reroutes. Hello, {reroute}. You can't cancel a need. You can only transfer it.

**Beat 3 (second click):** And when the dam breaks — *"I failed again"* — that isn't just a defeat. It's fuel. Shame is the engine, and willpower keeps feeding it.

**Body:** This is why trying harder makes it stronger. Willpower attacks the behavior, but the engine is the unmet need plus the shame. The man who wrote that sentence in AD 57 — his name was Paul — spent a whole chapter on these exact mechanics: the harder the rules pushed, the harder the loop pulled. "Who will rescue me?" he finally asked. Hold that question.
**Ref:** Romans 7

## Act 4.5 — The respectable loop

**H2:** Fine. Then you'll upgrade.

**Body:** If the obvious exits are traps, take the respectable one: try harder. Get disciplined. Get impressive. Get spiritual, even. *Earn* your peace.

**Interactive — the acceptance meter:** [ Achieve something ]
**Feed lines (cycled per click):** The grades. / The promotion. / The marathon. / They clapped. / "You've changed!" / A perfect streak.
**Revealed after a few clicks:** The bar never stays full. The baseline rises. Psychology has a name for this: the hedonic treadmill.

**Diagram relabels to:** enough? → prove it → perform → applause → wears off

**Body:** Look at the diagram. Same circle. The respectable loop runs on the identical engine — *am I enough?* — it's just slower, and people applaud it. There's an old story about two sons: one wasted his life in a far country, one stayed home and earned, resentfully, perfectly. The famous part is that the wild one was lost. The sting is that the obedient one was too. The most dangerous loops are the ones that look like virtue.

**Kicker:** Even religion runs this loop, given the chance. The earning experiment has been run for centuries under ideal conditions — the best rules, the most devoted people. The lab report is unanimous: the circle doesn't break from inside.

## Act 5 — The exit

**H2:** The circle has a name.

**Body:** Five hundred years ago, Martin Luther defined sin — not as rule-breaking, but as a shape: *incurvatus in se*. The self, curved in on itself. Look at the diagram one more time. Every strategy so far — numbing, willpower, earning — kept you at the center, managing yourself. The loop was never one bad arrow. It's the curve.

**Body:** Which is why the exit isn't an improvement. It's a death and a gift. Three moves, and none of them are yours:

**Move one — the engine is removed.** "There is now no condemnation." Not less. *None.* Shame isn't managed; it's de-powered.
**Ref:** Romans 8:1

**Move two — the need is finally met.** Not at the vending machine — at the source. "Abide in me… apart from me you can do nothing." The ache of {need} was never too big. It was aimed at something too small.
**Ref:** John 15

**Move three — the self is released.** "Deny yourself" isn't self-hatred. It's resignation from the exhausting job of being your own project. "Whoever loses his life will find it."
**Ref:** Matthew 16:24–25

**The brain-break:** The Greek word for repentance is *metanoia* — literally, a changed mind. Not groveling; groveling is just earning with worse posture. The brain-break is switching from earn to receive. And your brain will fight it — an unconditional gift trips every alarm you have. *What's the catch?* The catch was nailed up once, with the ledger. The ancient receipt-word *tetelestai* — paid in full — was the last thing he said.
**Ref:** John 19:30 · Colossians 2:14

**Interactive:** [ I can't run this one — receive it ]
**Caveat above button:** You've pressed every other button on this page. This one is different. You can't run grace. You can only receive it.

**(After — the circle breaks open, the page floods with light)**

**Body (in the light):** Honesty, before you scroll on: this isn't a button in real life either. People who believe this still fall. The loop isn't broken when the falling stops — it's broken when the fall stops feeding the engine. Shame hides. Grace returns. That difference is everything: same stumble, opposite direction.

## Act 6 — The spiral

**H2:** The same rhythm, opened upward.

**Body:** You don't stop being a creature of loops — you were built rhythmic. Triggers still come. The difference is the route: the ache, brought to the Father instead of hidden from him. The need, met at a source that doesn't run dry. Trust, a little deeper than last time. Around and around — but now each pass ascends.

**Stage caption:** the ache → brought, not hidden → met at the source → trust deepens → fruit

**Body:** And what grows along the spiral isn't produced. It's *grown*: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control. Notice the category. Fruit, not works. You can't manufacture a peach by trying hard at peaches; you plant something near water. "Be transformed by the renewing of your mind" turns out to be a neurologically literal sentence: the old road isn't erased — a new road is built, and traveled, until it's the default.
**Ref:** Galatians 5:22–23 · Romans 12:2

**Contrast card:** The slave works *for* standing. The son works *from* standing. Same work — opposite direction. From "so that I'll be accepted" to "because I already am."
**Ref:** Romans 8:15

**Epigraph:** "Grace is not opposed to effort; it is opposed to earning." — Dallas Willard

## Act 7 — Walking it out

**H2:** What to actually do at 1 a.m.

**Practice 1 — Bring the trigger, not the apology.** The old pattern prays after the fall. The new one brings the ache while it's still an ache: *I'm feeling it. It's about {need}. I'm bringing it to you instead.* That's abiding — mid-loop, not post-mortem.

**Practice 2 — Drag the shame into the light.** Shame is anaerobic — it only survives sealed. One safe person who knows the real story cuts its food supply in half. "Confess to one another… and be healed."
**Ref:** James 5:16

**Practice 3 — Replace; don't just resist.** The road doesn't unbuild. Build the other one: when the pull comes, have somewhere to drive — a walk, a psalm, a call, a kitchen you're allowed in. Put off, put on.
**Ref:** Ephesians 4:22–24

**Practice 4 — Feed the prediction machine.** Trust isn't a leap into the dark; it's a leap onto a track record. Remember, write down, retell every time the ache was met. That isn't sentimentality — it's training data. "He who did not spare his own Son — how will he not also…?"
**Ref:** Romans 8:32

**The honest caveat:** Some loops are also medical. Addiction with a body of its own, trauma, OCD, depression — these need more than a website, and seeking therapy or treatment is not a failure of faith. Grace and therapy aren't rivals: one works on the pathways, the other works on the engine.
